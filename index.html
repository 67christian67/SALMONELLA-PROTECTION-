SALMONELLA PROTECTION

Essential, science-backed strategies to stop one of the most common preventable foodborne threats — updated with latest CDC guidance.

What is Salmonella?

Salmonella bacteria cause salmonellosis, a major foodborne illness. These Gram-negative rods thrive in animal intestines and contaminate food through fecal matter. Per CDC (as of 2025–2026 data), it's still among the top causes of bacterial food poisoning in the US.

Symptoms usually start 6–72 hours after exposure:
• Diarrhea (often bloody)
• Fever >101°F
• Severe cramps
• Vomiting, nausea
Lasts 4–7 days for most; seek care for high fever, bloody stools, dehydration, or if high-risk.

High-risk groups (per CDC):

  • Children under 5
  • Adults 65+
  • Immunocompromised (e.g., diabetes, cancer treatment)
  • Pregnant people

How It Spreads

Primarily fecal-oral: contaminated food/water or contact with animals/handlers.

  • Undercooked poultry, eggs, meat
  • Cross-contamination in kitchens
  • Unpasteurized dairy/juices
  • Contaminated produce (sprouts, melons)
  • Reptiles, baby poultry contact
  • Poor hand hygiene

Prevention – CDC's Core 4 Steps

Follow **Clean, Separate, Cook, Chill** religiously — these interrupt transmission at every point (latest CDC guidelines emphasize thermometer use and no rinsing raw poultry).

CLEAN • SEPARATE • COOK • CHILL
  • CLEAN: 20+ sec handwashing; sanitize surfaces/utensils after raw items. Never rinse raw chicken (aerosolizes bacteria).
  • SEPARATE: Dedicated boards; raw meats bottom fridge shelf.
  • COOK: Thermometer — poultry/ground meat 165°F (74°C); eggs fully firm. Avoid raw egg recipes unless pasteurized.
  • CHILL: Refrigerate <2 hrs; fridge ≤40°F. Safe thawing only.
  • Avoid unpasteurized for vulnerable groups.
  • Don't cook if symptomatic.

Key Takeaway

Raw animal products = potential hazard. Treat them accordingly.

These habits prevent most cases. Small actions protect families, especially kids and elderly.
